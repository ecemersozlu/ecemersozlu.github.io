Speaking for the Firewall: Mainstream Moralization of Democracy

Presented at EPSS 2026

This paper examines how mainstream parties in Western Europe moralize democracy in response to radical right electoral pressure.

Many elites claim that European democracy is under attack and should be protected. When does defending democracy become salient in a consolidated democracy? As competitive pressure and voter grievances mount, how do parties balance their issue agendas with debating democracy? I argue that the success of nativist anti-establishment parties in Western Europe prompts dominant parties to moralize democracy under agenda constraints. Radical right popularity, as a reminder of authoritarian history, activates the moral aversion that underpins the firewall (cordon sanitaire) of non-cooperation with extremist parties. Using moral dictionaries and stem searches benchmarked against ManifestoBERTa, I analyze parliamentary debate corpora from Germany, the Netherlands, Sweden, and the United Kingdom, 1990–2019, to construct a longitudinal panel of moral rhetoric in parties’ democracy-related speech. The findings show that, when the radical right challenger is doing well in electoral polls, parties with governing experience do not mention democracy more but instead start framing it as a matter of good and evil. Parties' moral language around democracy further increases with the effective number of issues in their agenda, pointing to a need for more emotional and persuasive language to effectively get their message across among competing signals. The same response does not appear in 1990--2019 parliamentary debates when radical left challengers or mainstream right parties become more popular, suggesting that the effect is not a generic response to challenger pressure or right-wing electoral strength. The findings show that while parliamentary parties do not necessarily truncate their issue agendas to defend democratic norms, they still respond to the implications of far-right viability.

Always Principled, Sometimes Democratic: How Moral Convictions Guide Policy–Democracy Trade-offs

Draft, 2026

Using open-ended surveys and an experiment, both administered with LLM assistance in Germany, this paper investigates which democratic norms individuals associate with their moral principals and how morals affect potential policy--democracy trade-offs.

Voter Priorities and Mainstream Party Perceptions Under Populist Challenge

Presented at MPSA 2025

This paper examines how perceived democratic threat from radical populist parties leads mainstream voters to blur ideological differences between established parties and assimilate those parties’ positions toward their own.

In Western European multi-party democracies, the electoral success of radical populist parties (RPPs) challenges the status quo, disrupting party systems and patterns of representation. When the populist opponent is perceived as threat to democracy, how do voters balance strategic alliances with their own demands? I argue that this threat shifts electoral competition from the traditional left--right cleavage to a mainstream--populist divide in multi-party elections. When voters want to defeat a perceived threat, they shift from policy-based evaluations to strategic, identity-driven reasoning. This process affects party perceptions in two ways: (1) voters become less attentive to ideological distinctions between mainstream parties (``blurring"), and (2) they perceive mainstream parties as ideologically closer to themselves than they actually are (``assimilating"). I use panel data from the German Longitudinal Election Study to examine ideological perceptions and democratic satisfaction from 2017 to 2021. The findings show that mainstream voters who perceive RRPs as a threat to democracy are more likely to strategically distort their perception of mainstream party positions. Under this democratic threat, voters who prioritize keeping out the far-right are more likely to pull mainstream parties' ideological positions closer to their own. On the other hand, the perception of parties' ideological proximity is more resistant to change in individuals who prioritize policy outcomes. This makes blurring by avoiding information on mainstream parties' policy platforms more likely. These dynamics have implications for the downstream consequences of populist success and the electorate's ability to demand policy representation.
